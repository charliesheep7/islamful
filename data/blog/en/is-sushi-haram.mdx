## Is Sushi Haram? The Ruling

**Sushi made with fish is halal.** All four major schools of Islamic jurisprudence — Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali — agree that fish is permissible. It does not require ritual slaughter, and there is **no Islamic requirement to cook it**. Eating raw fish is perfectly fine.

However, sushi is not just fish. The rice, sauces, and other ingredients can introduce haram elements. The two main concerns are **mirin (rice wine) in sushi rice** and **non-fish seafood** that some schools restrict.

> **Quick Answer:** Fish-based sushi is halal. Watch out for mirin (alcohol) in the rice seasoning, and if you follow the Hanafi school, be aware that non-fish seafood (shrimp, crab, eel) is a point of scholarly disagreement.

## The Evidence from Quran and Sunnah

The permissibility of seafood is established clearly in the Quran. Allah (SWT) says:

**أُحِلَّ لَكُمْ صَيْدُ الْبَحْرِ وَطَعَامُهُ مَتَاعًا لَّكُمْ وَلِلسَّيَّارَةِ**

_Uhilla lakum saydul-bahri wa ta'amuhu mata'an lakum wa lis-sayyarah_

"Lawful to you is what you catch from the sea and its food as provision for you and for travelers." ([Surah Al-Ma'idah, 5:96](https://quran.com/5/96))

The word **طَعَام** (_ta'am_, food) here refers to what the sea provides — and the majority of scholars understand this to include all sea creatures, whether caught alive or found dead. This is a broad permission that covers fish served raw, cooked, or in any other form.

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) further affirmed this regarding the sea:

**هُوَ الطَّهُورُ مَاؤُهُ الْحِلُّ مَيْتَتُهُ**

_Huwa at-tahuru ma'uhu al-hillu maytatuh_

"Its water is pure and its dead are halal." ([Narrated by Abu Dawud, 83](https://sunnah.com/abudawud:83); [Tirmidhi, 69](https://sunnah.com/tirmidhi:69))

This hadith is significant because it establishes that sea creatures **do not require slaughter**. A fish that dies in the water or on the boat is still halal — unlike land animals, which must be slaughtered according to Islamic guidelines. This is why raw fish in sushi carries no issue from the perspective of slaughter or preparation method.

Regarding the prohibition of alcohol — which is relevant to the mirin concern — Allah says:

**يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا إِنَّمَا الْخَمْرُ وَالْمَيْسِرُ وَالْأَنصَابُ وَالْأَزْلَامُ رِجْسٌ مِّنْ عَمَلِ الشَّيْطَانِ فَاجْتَنِبُوهُ**

_Ya ayyuhalladhina amanu innamal-khamru wal-maysiru wal-ansabu wal-azlamu rijsun min 'amalish-shaytani fajtanibuhu_

"O you who have believed, indeed intoxicants, gambling, stone altars, and divining arrows are but defilement from the work of Satan, so avoid them." ([Surah Al-Ma'idah, 5:90](https://quran.com/5/90))

Mirin is a rice wine with roughly 14% alcohol content. It is classified as **khamr** (intoxicant) and is therefore haram as an ingredient.

## Scholar Opinions on Seafood and Sushi

The schools of jurisprudence differ on which types of sea creatures are permissible. This matters for sushi because many rolls contain shrimp, crab, eel, or octopus alongside fish.

### Madhab comparison for seafood

| Sea Creature              | Hanafi                    | Maliki    | Shafi'i   | Hanbali   |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------- | --------- | --------- | --------- |
| Fish (salmon, tuna, etc.) | **Halal**                 | **Halal** | **Halal** | **Halal** |
| Shrimp/Prawns             | **Debated** (many permit) | **Halal** | **Halal** | **Halal** |
| Crab/Lobster              | **Not permissible**       | **Halal** | **Halal** | **Halal** |
| Eel                       | **Not permissible**       | **Halal** | **Halal** | **Halal** |
| Octopus/Squid             | **Not permissible**       | **Halal** | **Halal** | **Halal** |

The **Hanafi school** restricts permissible sea creatures to **fish only** (_samak_) — animals with scales that live exclusively in water. Under this view, crustaceans (shrimp, crab, lobster), mollusks (octopus, squid), and scaleless creatures (eel) are not permissible or at minimum disliked (_makruh_). Many contemporary Hanafi scholars do permit shrimp, but the stricter position excludes it. For a deeper look at the ruling on crustaceans, see our article on [is crab haram](/blog/is-crab-haram).

The **Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali schools** take a broader view. They hold that **all sea creatures are halal** based on the general permission in Surah Al-Ma'idah 5:96 and the hadith about the sea. Under these schools, sushi containing any type of seafood is permissible.

For a broader understanding of how Islamic dietary categories work, see our guide on [halal vs haram](/blog/halal-vs-haram).

## Conditions and Gray Areas

**Mirin in sushi rice.** This is the most common issue. Traditional Japanese sushi rice is seasoned with a mixture of rice vinegar, sugar, and mirin. Since mirin contains alcohol, sushi rice prepared this way is **not halal**. Many halal-conscious sushi restaurants substitute mirin with additional rice vinegar or sugar. Always ask.

**Soy sauce.** Naturally brewed soy sauce contains trace amounts of alcohol from fermentation (typically 1-2%). Most scholars consider these negligible trace amounts permissible because they do not intoxicate and occur naturally. However, if you prefer to be cautious, tamari or halal-certified soy sauce is available. For more on how [alcohol in food](/blog/is-alcohol-haram) is treated in Islamic law, see our detailed article.

**Cross-contamination at restaurants.** Non-halal sushi restaurants may prepare pork-based dishes (like tonkatsu rolls) or use shared cutting boards and utensils. The risk of cross-contamination with haram ingredients exists. If you are eating at a non-halal establishment, ask about preparation practices and whether your sushi can be prepared on a clean surface.

**Imitation crab (surimi).** California rolls use imitation crab, which is actually processed fish (usually pollock). This is halal, though you should check for added ingredients like [gelatin](/blog/is-gelatin-haram) or other non-halal binders in some brands.

## Common Questions

### Is raw fish halal?

**Yes.** There is no Islamic requirement to cook fish. The permissibility of fish comes from its nature as a sea creature, not from how it is prepared. Sashimi, ceviche, and raw fish in sushi are all permissible. The only consideration is whether eating it raw poses a **health risk** — if a particular raw fish is known to be unsafe, avoiding it would fall under the general Islamic principle of not harming yourself.

### Can I eat sushi at a non-halal Japanese restaurant?

You can, but with caution. Stick to **fish-based rolls**, ask whether the rice contains mirin, and inquire about cross-contamination with pork products. Many Japanese restaurants serve tonkatsu (pork cutlet) and other pork dishes, so shared fryers or prep surfaces are a legitimate concern.

### Is vegetable sushi halal?

**Yes.** Vegetable rolls (cucumber, avocado, sweet potato) are halal as long as the rice does not contain mirin. This is often the safest option at a non-halal restaurant.

## Summary

**Fish-based sushi is halal according to all four schools of Islamic jurisprudence.** Fish does not require slaughter, and eating it raw is permissible. The areas of concern are the **mirin (rice wine) in sushi rice**, which must be avoided or substituted, and **non-fish seafood** like shrimp, crab, and eel, which the Hanafi school restricts to fish only while the other three schools permit broadly.

When eating sushi, **ask about the rice preparation**, choose fish-based options if you follow the Hanafi school, and be mindful of cross-contamination at non-halal restaurants.
