**Trick or treating is haram according to the majority of Islamic scholars.** It is a core Halloween activity, and Halloween itself is rooted in pagan traditions. Participating in trick-or-treating falls under the Islamic prohibition of _tashabbuh_ — imitating non-Muslims in their religious and cultural festivals.

The ruling is not about candy. It is about what trick-or-treating represents: active participation in a celebration that has no place in Islamic practice.

> **Quick Answer:** Trick or treating is haram. It is inseparable from Halloween, which originates from pagan traditions (Samhain) and violates the principle of tashabbuh. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said, "Whoever imitates a people is one of them" (Abu Dawud, 4031). Muslims should avoid trick-or-treating, Halloween costumes, and related festivities.

## The Evidence From Quran and Hadith

Trick-or-treating does not exist in a vacuum. It is the most recognizable act of Halloween participation — going door to door in costume, collecting treats as part of a communal celebration. Scholars who rule it haram do so based on two main bodies of evidence.

First, the hadith on imitating other nations:

> **"مَنْ تَشَبَّهَ بِقَوْمٍ فَهُوَ مِنْهُمْ"**
>
> _Man tashabbaha bi qawmin fa huwa minhum_
>
> "Whoever imitates a people is one of them." — Narrated by Abu Dawud, [4031](https://sunnah.com/abudawud:4031)

This hadith is the cornerstone of the tashabbuh principle. Trick-or-treating is not a neutral, universal activity — it is specific to Halloween, a festival with [pagan Celtic origins](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Halloween) (Samhain) later absorbed into Christian observance (All Hallows' Eve). Participating in its signature ritual counts as imitating non-Muslims in their distinctive customs.

Second, the hadith on Islamic celebrations:

> The Prophet (peace be upon him) arrived in Madinah and found the people celebrating two days of festivities. He said: **"Allah (SWT) has replaced them for you with two days that are better: Eid al-Adha and Eid al-Fitr."** — Narrated by Abu Dawud, [1134](https://sunnah.com/abudawud:1134)

This hadith establishes that Islam has its own celebrations. Adding festivals from other traditions — or actively participating in them — introduces practices that have no basis in the religion. For a deeper look at how the full Halloween ruling works, see our article on [is Halloween haram](/blog/is-halloween-haram).

The Quran reinforces this:

> وَلَا تَتَّبِعُوا السُّبُلَ فَتَفَرَّقَ بِكُمْ عَنْ سَبِيلِهِ
>
> _Wa lā tattabiʿū al-subula fatafarraqa bikum ʿan sabīlihi_
>
> "And do not follow [other] ways, for they will separate you from His way." — Surah Al-An'am, [6:153](https://quran.com/6/153)

## What Scholars Say

The scholarly position on trick-or-treating mirrors the broader [Halloween ruling](/blog/is-halloween-haram), because trick-or-treating is not a standalone act — it is Halloween's central ritual.

| Scholar / Institution                        | Ruling on Trick-or-Treating | Reasoning                                                     |
| -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| IslamQA (Sheikh Salih al-Munajjid)           | Haram                       | Direct participation in a pagan-origin festival               |
| Dar al-Ulum (AskImam)                        | Haram                       | Tashabbuh — imitating non-Muslims in their festivals          |
| Dr. Muzammil Siddiqi (ISNA)                  | Haram                       | Pagan roots; practices contradict tawhid                      |
| Assembly of Muslim Jurists of America (AMJA) | Haram                       | Halloween participation in all forms prohibited               |
| Dar al-Ifta al-Misriyyah (Egypt)             | Avoid (cautious)            | Distinguish between active celebration and incidental contact |

The majority position is clear: trick-or-treating is haram because it constitutes active participation in Halloween. You are not just eating candy — you are walking through the streets as part of a communal ritual tied to a non-Islamic celebration.

The [AMJA](https://www.amjaonline.org/) has specifically addressed the question of Muslim children trick-or-treating and concluded it is impermissible. Sheikh al-Munajjid's [fatwa on IslamQA](https://islamqa.info/en/answers/45200) addresses the broader principle of participating in non-Muslim holidays, which directly applies here.

Dar al-Ifta al-Misriyyah offers the most nuanced position, distinguishing between active celebration and incidental social contact. Even in this minority view, dressing up and trick-or-treating would be discouraged.

## Conditions and Gray Areas

The prohibition is strongest when:

- **You or your children dress in costumes and go door to door** — this is the definition of trick-or-treating and the most direct form of participation
- **The activity is framed as celebrating Halloween** — even without costumes, joining a Halloween-themed event is problematic
- **Children are taught that trick-or-treating is normal** — scholars emphasize that normalizing non-Islamic celebrations for children is a long-term concern

The gray areas scholars discuss include:

- **Accepting candy passively** — if a neighbor offers your child candy without any Halloween framing, some scholars permit accepting it as a simple gift. Gift-giving is encouraged in Islam on its own terms.
- **School events** — if a school requires participation, scholars advise explaining your position respectfully and seeking an exemption. Many schools accommodate religious objections.
- **Secular alternatives** — some Muslim families host "harvest gatherings" or family dinners in late October as a positive alternative. Scholars generally support redirecting children toward halal activities rather than simply saying no. Keeping up with your [daily prayers](/prayer-times) and worship is a better focus for that evening.

The distinction always comes back to intention and context. For more on how Islam draws the line between halal and haram in daily life, see our [halal vs haram guide](/blog/halal-vs-haram).

## Common Questions

**Q: My kids feel left out when everyone else is trick-or-treating. What should I do?**

This is one of the most common concerns Muslim parents face. Scholars advise explaining — in age-appropriate terms — why your family does things differently, and providing alternatives. A special family outing, a movie night, or a small gathering with other Muslim families can help. The goal is not to punish your children but to teach them that Muslims have their own identity and celebrations. Building that identity is more effective than a blanket ban with no explanation.

**Q: Is buying Halloween candy on sale after October 31 haram?**

No. The candy itself is not haram (assuming it contains [halal ingredients](/blog/is-gelatin-haram)). The prohibition is on participating in the celebration, not on consuming products that happen to be associated with it. Buying discounted candy after the holiday is a separate transaction with no connection to the festival.

**Q: Is trick-or-treating worse than other forms of Halloween participation?**

Scholars do not typically rank Halloween activities from "less haram" to "more haram." However, trick-or-treating is the most visible and active form of participation — you are literally walking through the community as part of the celebration. It is harder to argue incidental contact when you are in costume going door to door.

## Summary

Trick or treating is **haram** according to the majority of Islamic scholars. The ruling rests on:

- **Tashabbuh** — the prohibition on imitating non-Muslims in their distinctive festivals, based on the hadith "Whoever imitates a people is one of them" (Abu Dawud, 4031)
- **Halloween's pagan origins** — trick-or-treating is the central ritual of a celebration rooted in Samhain and All Hallows' Eve
- **Quranic guidance** to follow the straight path and not follow other ways (Surah Al-An'am, 6:153)

The prohibition applies to dressing in costumes, going door to door, and participating in Halloween-themed events. Gray areas exist around passive situations like accepting a gift from a neighbor, but the default ruling is to avoid trick-or-treating entirely.
